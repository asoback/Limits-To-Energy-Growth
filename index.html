Here's how much oil the US Government [1] says that the whole world has used since the 80s. The data is indexed, so that 100 is the total amount of energy consumed by all of humanity in the year 1980. 200 would be twice that amount of energy, like we see around 2014. Persumable, if the amount of energy consumed doubles every 34 years, then it would have been gotten a 50 in 1946, and would reach 400 by 2050.

Other sources to check for this would be the BP statistical review of world energy data use.

When scientists[1], the government[2], or others say things like "at current rates of use, we have about 52 years of oil left" Or how we at current rates of use, coal would last another 130 year, and over 50 for natural gas, this is what that looks like as a graph. Of course, the supply of renewables is assumed to grow. This isn't exactly a likely scenario.

%

1.4% is what is generally expected [citation needed].

We can make a few assumptions about the future, and improve this model.

  • Renewables continue to grow
  • Demand continues to grow (The pink line)
  • Non-renewables will grow for some time, peak, then decline
Here there is a problem. Significantly more demand than supply. What will this mean for the cost of energy, the availability of energy around the globe, infrastructure where we live? Does this have to be a problem? Yes. The reason is that as coal, natural gas, and oil run out, in order for nuclear and renewables to keep up with growing demand, they would have to not only keep up with their own growth, they'd have to make up for the missing fossil fuel growth, and replace the fossil fuel consumption as it drops. The growth required of renewables and nuclear would be absolutely massive. Larger than any project ever completed by humanity type massive. In fact, the growth required is likely larger than is even possible.

Let's explore some of those assumptions

Population

Some people think the population will just level out at 10 Billion in just a few short years.

Others think maybe it'll get to 11, or 12 Billion.

Some people think we'll overshoot and our population will have to fall back down to something more sustainable.

Still some think our Earthly population could skyrocket much higher.

The population stabalizes around year ...

If there is no forseable limit, I won't even show it on a chart. Just play with this compound interest calculator.

Once we reach the Earth's carrying capacity and our population stabalizes our per capita energy may continue to grow, until perhaps that balances out as well.

Right now the average person uses around 90 Million BTUs per year

The average American, in contrast, uses like 21 times that per year[citation needed]

Compare standard of living to the average person today. The average person today gets a 1. if we bring every person up to the US standard of living, that is 21. If we double everyones energy use, globally that is 42, etc.

Where this starts is a bad scenario. The rich get go richer, the poor get no poorer, energy wise. If there is a change, the rich and the poor either gravitate towards some middle ground, or perhaps inequality becomes even greater.

See what happens when you double that number. See what it looks like if everyone moves towards the American standard of living (21).

If you haven't noticed, my point is that the expectation of continued growth forever and always is completely absurd...

Undiscovered Resources

The amount of remaining resources so far that we've been discussing are what oil companies are 90% confident we will be able to find and retrieve. Of course, there may be more that we are able to dig up under the oceans, and climate change melting the polar regions of the planet will pave the way for even more fossil fuel discoveries.

It's also worth considering that we won't use every last drop as the climate crisis develops and as our civilization decides to transition to a sustainable future.

Conclusion

Exponential energy growth forever without breaks is flat out impossible. Exponential population growth forever without breaks is flat out impossible. Obviously, exponential improvements in technological efficiency forever is also impossible, as a transister can only get so small, a solar panel can only get so efficient, etc. Understanding both of those should make it easy to see that services per person, productivity per person, and other factors that influence economic growth are also subject to very real, fast approaching limits.

As a global civilization, we will either hit plateaus, or collapses. A collapse is obviously bad, but a plateau does not have to be. It can be intentional. We can make it work for us by changing our economic system to one build around the idea of "steady state".
